Ella Fitzgerald-Biography

  • Intro
  • Who?

Ella Fitzgerald, also known as the "First Lady of Song" and the the "Queen of Jazz,", was an American jazz vocalist born on April 25, 1917, in Newport News, Virginia. With a career spanning over six decades, Fitzgerald became one of the most celebrated and influential jazz singers of all time.

Known for her impeccable voice, scat singing, and improvisational skills, Fitzgerald recorded numerous albums and worked with legendary musicians such as Duke Ellington, Louis Armstrong, and Count Basie. Her contributions to the world of jazz and her extraordinary vocal talent have left an indelible mark on the music industry.

Throughout her career, Ella Fitzgerald received numerous awards, including 13 Grammy Awards. She passed away on June 15, 1996, but her legacy continues to inspire and influence generations of musicians.

Who Was Ella Fitzgerald?

Ella Fitzgerald turned to singing after a troubled childhood and debuted at the Apollo Theater in 1934. Discovered in an amateur contest, she went on to become the top female jazz singer for decades. In 1958, Fitzgerald made history as the first African American woman to win a Grammy Award. Due in no small part to her vocal quality, with lucid intonation and a broad range, the singer would go on to win 13 Grammys in total and sell more than 40 million albums. Her multi-volume "songbooks" on Verve Records are among America's recording treasures.
